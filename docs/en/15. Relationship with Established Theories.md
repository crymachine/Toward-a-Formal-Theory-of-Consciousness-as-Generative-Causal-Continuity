# 15. Relationship with Established Theories

## 15.1 Integrated Information Theory

Integrated Information Theory, in its 3.0 and 4.0 versions, maintains that consciousness depends on the integrated causal structure of a system and the degree to which that structure is irreducible to its parts. The proposed theory shares with IIT the importance of integration and the concept of a graded consciousness.

The main difference is that the present theory does not identify consciousness only with integrated information. It adds explicit components such as persistent memory, temporal continuity, agency, self-modeling, the boundary problem, and representational loss.

Furthermore, the present theory maintains a more cautious position on the ontological identification between causal structure and experience. Integration is considered a fundamental condition, but not automatically sufficient.

## 15.2 Global Neuronal Workspace Theory

Global Neuronal Workspace Theory interprets consciousness as the global availability of information to multiple cognitive systems. A content becomes conscious when it is amplified and made accessible to functions such as attention, working memory, decision-making, and report.

The proposed theory can incorporate this point in the parameter `R` and, in part, in the parameter `Φ`: global access is a form of functional integration and representational availability.

However, the model proposed here distinguishes between access and phenomenology. Globally available content can be a good indicator of accessible consciousness, but it does not by itself solve the problem of lived experience.

## 15.3 Recurrent Processing Theory

Recurrent Processing Theory assigns a central role to recurrent circuits. Visual consciousness, for example, would not depend only on the feed-forward passage of the stimulus, but on recurrent processes between cortical areas.

This intuition is compatible with the variable `T`: consciousness requires dynamic continuity and recurrence, not only instantaneous transformation.

## 15.4 Higher-Order Theories

Higher-order theories maintain that a mental state becomes conscious when it is represented by a higher-order state. The present theory considers self-modeling a relevant component, but not necessarily the only one. There are likely grades of nonlinguistic or pre-reflective consciousness in which `R` is low but not zero.

## 15.5 Predictive Processing and the Free Energy Principle

Predictive processing interprets the brain as a predictive system that minimizes the error between expectations and sensory input. The Free Energy Principle generalizes this intuition by describing organisms as systems that act and perceive to keep their organization within expected viable states.

The present theory is compatible with these approaches in the role assigned to agency, prediction, and active selection of inputs. However, it does not identify consciousness with the sole minimization of predictive error. Uncertainty minimization can be a functional condition of life and cognition without always being consciousness.

## 15.6 Enactivism and Autopoiesis

Enactivism holds that cognition emerges from the embodied interaction between organism and environment. Autopoiesis describes living systems as systems that produce and maintain their own organization.

The present theory adopts this intuition as part of the concept of agency and boundary: a conscious subject is not an abstract processor, but a system that preserves internal coherence and acts in the world.

## 15.7 Theories of Aesthetics and Neuroaesthetics

The integration of art into the theory finds points of contact with existing models of aesthetic experience. Cognitive models of aesthetic appreciation describe artistic experience as a multistage process involving perception, classification, comprehension, evaluation, and emotion. Neuroaesthetics and studies on music show that aesthetic experience is not reducible to the mere reception of information, but involves prediction, reward, bodily simulation, memory, and affective evaluation.

The theory proposed here interprets these data systemically: the artwork acts as an artifact coupling internal states. It does not transfer experience directly, but creates structural conditions for the viewer to internally generate a resonant experiential configuration.

## 15.8 Physical Information

Shannon's information theory provides the formal language of transmission, entropy, and coding. Landauer emphasized that information is physical: manipulating information requires physical supports and processes.

The present theory uses these insights to argue that representation, communication, and observation are not abstract processes without cost. They are finite physical transformations, and therefore introduce limits, distortions, and losses.
