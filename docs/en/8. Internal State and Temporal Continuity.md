# 8. Internal State and Temporal Continuity

A theory of consciousness must assign a central role to continuity. A conscious system is not only an input-output function:

```math
Output = f(Input)
```

A conscious, or proto-conscious, system must at least be described as a dynamic system with an internal state:

```math
S_{t+1} = f(S_t, I_t)
```

where:

- `S_t` is the internal state at time `t`;
- `I_t` is the input received at time `t`;
- `S_{t+1}` is the subsequent internal state.

In this formulation, memory is not a passive archive. It is causal persistence. The system's past conditions the present, and the present conditions the future.

Temporal continuity can be approximated through the informational dependence between successive states:

```math
T_\Delta(S) = \frac{I(S_t; S_{t+\Delta})}{H(S_t)}
```

where:

- `I(S_t; S_{t+\Delta})` is the mutual information between the present state and the future state;
- `H(S_t)` is the entropy of the present state;
- `T_\Delta(S)` measures how much of the present remains causally relevant after an interval `Δ`.

A system with very low `T` changes in a fragmented or random way. A system with high `T` maintains dynamic coherence. Consciousness, in the proposed theory, requires at least a robust form of temporal continuity.
