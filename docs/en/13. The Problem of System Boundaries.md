# 13. The Problem of System Boundaries

A systemic theory of consciousness must answer a difficult question:

> where does a conscious system end?

The brain is not isolated from the body. The body is not isolated from the environment. Individuals communicate, cooperate, and influence one another. If consciousness depends on causal integration, why would a society not automatically be conscious? Why would the entire planet not be a mind? Why would the universe not be a single subject?

To avoid arbitrary conclusions, an additional variable is needed, which here is called `B`, from *boundary* or causal delimitation.

```math
C = f(M, \Phi, T, A, R, B)
```

`B` measures how much a system constitutes a relatively closed, coherent, and locally integrated causal unit with respect to the environment.

An intuitive formulation is:

```math
B(S) = \frac{I(S_t; S_{t+1})}{I(S_t; E_{t+1}) + \epsilon}
```

This measure compares the system's internal coherence with its immediate dependence on the environment. It does not mean that a conscious system must be isolated. It means that it must possess an internal dynamic sufficiently integrated to constitute its own trajectory.

This variable is crucial to distinguish between:

- an individual mind;
- a social group;
- an ecosystem;
- a digital network;
- the universe as a totality.

Without a boundary criterion, the theory risks sliding into indiscriminate panpsychism. With a boundary criterion, it can instead ask: which subsystem constitutes a local maximum of integration, continuity, and self-modeling?
