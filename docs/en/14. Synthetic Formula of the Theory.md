# 14. Synthetic Formula of the Theory

A possible normalized form of the theory is:

```math
C(S,t) = \sigma \left(
 w_M \hat{M}(S,t) +
 w_\Phi \hat{\Phi}(S,t) +
 w_T \hat{T}(S,t) +
 w_A \hat{A}(S,t) +
 w_R \hat{R}(S,t) +
 w_B \hat{B}(S,t)
\right)
```

where:

- `\hat{M}, \hat{\Phi}, \hat{T}, \hat{A}, \hat{R}, \hat{B}` are normalized variables;
- `w_i` are theoretical or empirically estimable weights;
- `\sigma` is a saturation function, for example logistic.

This additive form is useful for empirical research, but it may be conceptually too permissive. Some variables could be quasi-necessary conditions. For example, without temporal continuity or minimal integration, the other factors might not be enough.

An alternative, more constraining form is multiplicative:

```math
C(S,t) = K \cdot B \cdot T \cdot \Phi \cdot g(M,A,R)
```

This formula implies that if `B`, `T`, or `Φ` tend toward zero, then `C` also tends toward zero. It better represents the idea that consciousness requires an integrated and bounded trajectory.

The choice between additive, multiplicative, or hybrid form is an open empirical and theoretical question.
