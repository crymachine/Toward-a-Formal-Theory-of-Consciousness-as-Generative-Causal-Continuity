# 16. Consciousness, Simulation, and Ontological Identity

An artificial system can simulate language, emotions, empathy, and introspection. But behavioral simulation does not automatically imply subjective experience.

The fundamental distinction is:

```math
Observational\ equivalence \neq Ontological\ identity
```

Two systems can be indistinguishable from the outside with respect to a certain class of observations, but differ internally in their causal organization.

The proposed theory does not exclude that an artificial system could develop some degree of consciousness. On the contrary, if an artificial system possessed persistent memory, causal integration, temporal continuity, autonomous agency, self-modeling, and coherent dynamic boundaries, then the theory would predict an increase in `C`.

However, linguistic behavior alone is not enough. It is necessary to analyze the system's internal dynamics. A language model without autonomous state continuity, without persistent agency, and without strong causal self-preservation can simulate discourse about consciousness without thereby fully satisfying the theory's conditions.
