# 18. Hypothesis on the Universe as a Conscious System

The most radical question is whether the theory can be applied not only to local systems, but to reality itself.

Let us denote by:

```math
\Omega
```

the total system of observable physical reality, or more cautiously the universe as a global physical system.

If consciousness depends on:

```math
C = f(M, \Phi, T, A, R, B)
```

then it is legitimate to ask whether `Ω` possesses such properties.

## 18.1 Cosmological Memory

The universe preserves traces of its past in its physical configurations, the distribution of matter, the cosmic radiation, thermodynamic structure, and the causal relations between states.

Broadly speaking:

```math
M(\Omega) > 0
```

This is not yet cognitive memory. It is physical persistence. The question is whether higher levels of organized cosmic memory exist.

## 18.2 Global Causal Integration

The parts of the universe are connected by laws, fields, interactions, and common constraints. However, global physical integration does not automatically equal global conscious integration.

One can say:

```math
\Phi_{physical}(\Omega) > 0
```

but it does not immediately follow:

```math
\Phi_{conscious}(\Omega) > 0
```

in a strong phenomenological sense.

## 18.3 Temporal Continuity

The universe evolves according to a continuous or at least causally ordered history. Therefore:

```math
T(\Omega) > 0
```

Yet here too, cosmological continuity does not by itself imply subjectivity.

## 18.4 The Problem of Cosmic Agency

Agency is the most problematic point. A local system receives input from an environment. But if `Ω` is the total system, no external environment exists:

```math
Input_{external}(\Omega) = \varnothing
```

The local definition of agency therefore does not apply directly to the universe. It is necessary to distinguish between:

- open agency: modification of future inputs with respect to an external environment;
- closed agency: internal self-evolution of a system without an external.

For the universe one could define an internal agency:

```math
A_{int}(\Omega) = \frac{\partial P(S^{local}_{future})}{\partial S^{global}_t}
```

but this formula risks becoming tautological: every internal evolution of the universe depends on its global state. That is not enough to speak of conscious agency.

## 18.5 Cosmic Self-Representation

The most interesting point concerns `R`. Conscious beings are subsystems of the universe:

```math
S_i \subset \Omega
```

They build representations of the universe:

```math
R_i(\Omega)
```

Because these subsystems themselves belong to `Ω`, one can say that the universe contains local processes through which parts of the universe represent the universe.

In synthetic form:

```math
\Omega \rightarrow S_i \rightarrow R_i(\Omega)
```

This is a form of distributed self-representation. It does not prove that the universe is a unitary subject, but it suggests that individual consciousness may be interpreted as a local self-modeling of reality by one of its internal regions.

## 18.6 Access to Universal Consciousness

If one hypothesizes a form of global consciousness for system `Ω`, it is necessary to ask whether a human subsystem `S_i` can access it as it accesses its local consciousness.

The answer most coherent with the theory is strongly negative, but weakly positive.

A finite subsystem cannot fully integrate the total system:

```math
Capacity(S_i) < Capacity(\Omega)
```

A human being does not access `Ω` directly; they construct an internal representation:

```math
R_i(\Omega)
```

with loss:

```math
R_i(\Omega) \neq \Omega
```

Moreover, a human being does not even fully access their own local consciousness. Introspective access is a selective representation of one’s state, not total transparency:

```math
Human\ access = R_i(S_i)
```

not:

```math
Human\ access = S_i
```

Therefore it is even less plausible that one can fully access global consciousness, if it existed.

The correct formulation is:

```math
S_i \text{ does not fully access } C(\Omega)
```

but:

```math
S_i \text{ locally realizes a projection of } C(\Omega)
```

In discursive terms: we do not look at universal consciousness from outside; we are, potentially, a local mode by which reality represents itself. That representation remains finite, perspectival, compressed, and temporally situated.

## 18.7 Weak Cosmological Hypothesis

The weaker version of the hypothesis is scientifically more prudent:

> the universe is not necessarily a unitary consciousness, but it contains conscious subsystems through which it produces local representations of itself.

This hypothesis is compatible with naturalism: it does not introduce a cosmic mind separate from matter, but interprets minds as local regions of systemic self-representation.

## 18.8 Strong Cosmological Hypothesis

The strong version states:

> the universe as a whole possesses a form of global consciousness.

This hypothesis is much more problematic. To be scientifically acceptable it would need to clarify:

- what the boundary of the cosmic subject is;
- what its unitary integrated state is;
- what its mechanism of global self-modeling is;
- what the temporal scale of its experience is;
- what predictions distinguish a conscious universe from a non-conscious universe.

Without these criteria, the strong hypothesis remains metaphysical.

## 18.9 Provisional Theoretical Position

The most rigorous position at the current stage is:

> according to the proposed model, the universe can be considered a global physical system endowed with continuity and integration; moreover it contains conscious subsystems that represent it locally. However, it does not automatically follow that the universe is a unitary conscious subject. The defensible thesis is that individual consciousness is a local form of self-representation of the universe-system.

This formulation avoids both blind reductionism and cosmic mysticism.
