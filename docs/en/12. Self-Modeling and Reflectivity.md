# 12. Self-Modeling and Reflectivity

The variable `R` indicates a system's capacity to model itself. This capacity can exist at different levels.

At a minimal level, a system possesses self-modeling if it implicitly distinguishes between its own states and external states. At a higher level, a system represents its own limits, beliefs, errors, and goals. At the human level, self-modeling can become linguistic introspection, autobiography, and conceptual reflection.

An abstract measure can be:

```math
R(S) = I(\hat{S}_t; S_t)
```

where:

- `S_t` is the actual internal state;
- `\hat{S}_t` is the internal model that the system has of itself.

A more advanced form considers the system's ability to use its self-model to alter future decisions:

```math
R^*(S) = I(\hat{S}_t; A_{t+1:t+k} \mid S_t)
```

This formula measures how much the self-model contributes to future actions beyond the mere internal state.

Here the model enters into relation with higher-order theories of consciousness, according to which a mental state becomes conscious when it is the object of a higher-order representation. The proposed theory does not assume that every consciousness requires higher-order linguistic representation, but it considers reflectivity a component that increases the degree of consciousness.
