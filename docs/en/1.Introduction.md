# 1. Introduction

The problem of consciousness remains one of the most resilient knots in the philosophy of mind, cognitive neuroscience, and systems theory. Part of the difficulty comes from the fact that the term “consciousness” is used to refer to different phenomena: wakefulness, cognitive access, the ability to report a mental state, subjective experience, sense of self, intentionality, autobiographical memory, perceptual integration, and agency.

A scientific theory of consciousness must avoid two opposite mistakes. The first is to reduce consciousness too early to a single empirical indicator, for example verbal behavior or activity in a specific brain region. The second is to keep it as a purely metaphysical concept, impossible to measure, falsify, or compare with observable data.

The proposal developed in this document starts from an intermediate position: consciousness is treated as an emergent and graded property of dynamic systems endowed with internal continuity, causal integration, persistent memory, action capability, and self-modeling. It is not assumed that this definition immediately solves the phenomenological problem of subjective experience. Rather, it is assumed that it identifies a class of necessary, or at least highly relevant, conditions for the emergence of conscious phenomena.

The basic formulation is:

```math
C = f(M, \Phi, T, A, R)
```

where:

- `C` indicates the degree of consciousness of the system;
- `M` indicates persistent memory;
- `Φ` indicates causal integration;
- `T` indicates temporal continuity;
- `A` indicates agency, that is, the system's capacity to modify its future inputs;
- `R` indicates reflective or self-modeling capacity.

During the document, an additional boundary variable will also be introduced:

```math
B
```

which indicates the degree to which a system constitutes a causally delimitable unit relative to its environment.

The extended version of the function thus becomes:

```math
C = f(M, \Phi, T, A, R, B)
```

This function should not be interpreted, at the current stage, as a fully determined physical law. It is a theoretical structure to make progressively operational. The task of the theory is to specify what the variables mean, how they can be estimated, what predictions they generate, and under which conditions the model would fail.
