# 3. Main Thesis

The proposed thesis can be formulated as follows:

> Consciousness is the degree to which a delimitable dynamic system preserves an internally integrated continuity over time, uses that continuity to model itself and the environment, and actively modifies the distribution of its future inputs.

This thesis contains five fundamental claims.

The first claim is that consciousness is not binary. There is not necessarily a sharp boundary between a conscious system and a non-conscious system. There is rather a gradient:

```math
0 \leq C \leq 1
```

or, in more general form:

```math
C \in \mathbb{R}^{+}
```

The second claim is that consciousness requires temporal persistence. A system that produced only instantaneous outputs in response to instantaneous inputs, without preserving causal traces of its past, would not have experiential continuity.

The third claim is that consciousness requires integration. It is not enough for a system to have many parts or a lot of information: the parts must contribute to a unitary dynamic, not reducible to the sum of isolated processes.

The fourth claim is that consciousness grows with agency. A more conscious system does not simply receive inputs: it selects, induces, seeks, modifies, and structures its future inputs based on its internal state.

The fifth claim is that consciousness implies a form of self-modeling. Not necessarily in the full human sense of linguistic introspection, but at least in the minimal sense of an operative representation of its own state, its own limits, and its relation to the environment.
