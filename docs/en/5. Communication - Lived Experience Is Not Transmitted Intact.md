# 5. Communication: Lived Experience Is Not Transmitted Intact

If two conscious systems communicate, each has an internal state:

```math
S_t
```

Communication does not transfer `S_t` intact. It transmits a functional compression:

```math
C_m(S_t)
```

where `C_m` indicates a communicative encoding function. The message contains what can be expressed, selected, encoded, and transported through a channel.

When an individual says “I'm in pain,” the receiver does not receive the other's pain directly. They receive a linguistic signal, which they interpret through memory, empathy, inference, context, and learned models. The result is an internal reconstruction that is compatible, not the transfer of the original experience.

This leads to a fundamental distinction:

```math
experience \neq description(experience)
```

Human communication is functional, not consciousness itself. It allows coordination, prediction, and approximate sharing, but not integral transfer of the phenomenal state.

This distinction connects the theory to the classic problem of subjective experience: what Thomas Nagel formulated as “what it is like” to be a certain organism is not exhausted by the objective description of its physical or behavioral states.
