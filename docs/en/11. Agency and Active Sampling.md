# 11. Agency and Active Sampling

One of the central components of the theory is agency. A conscious system is not only traversed by inputs: it participates in the selection of its own future inputs.

In the passive model:

```math
I_t \rightarrow S_t \rightarrow O_t
```

the environment provides input, the system processes it, and produces output.

In the active model:

```math
S_t \leftrightarrow E_t
```

the system modifies the environment, or its position relative to the environment, influencing what it will be able to perceive in the future.

Agency can be formalized as the sensitivity of the distribution of future inputs to the current internal state:

```math
A = \frac{\partial P(I_{future})}{\partial S_t}
```

or, in informational terms:

```math
A_k(S) = I(S_t; I_{t+1:t+k} \mid E_t)
```

This measure indicates how much the current internal state contributes to the configuration of future inputs, given the same current environment.

A bacterium following a chemical gradient possesses minimal agency: it does not simply undergo the environment, but it modifies the probability of its future sensory states. An exploratory animal possesses greater agency. A human being who builds scientific instruments to observe otherwise invisible scales possesses even higher agency.

Agency is the point where the theory approaches active inference and the Free Energy Principle: an organism does not merely represent the world, but acts to reduce uncertainty, maintain internal coherence, and select states compatible with its own organization.
