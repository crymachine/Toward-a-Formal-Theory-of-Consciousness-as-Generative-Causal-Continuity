# 4. Observation, Representation, and Informational Loss

Every observation of a system implies a transformation. Observing does not mean absorbing the system in its entirety, but building a finite representation from available signals.

A real observational process always has:

- finite resolution;
- finite precision;
- noise threshold;
- latency;
- physical channel;
- selection criteria;
- interpretative apparatus.

A representation function can be defined as:

```math
O: X \rightarrow Y
```

where `X` is the space of the phenomenon's real states and `Y` is the space of representations available to the observer.

The crucial point is that `Y` is normally a compression of `X`. Representational loss can be indicated as:

```math
\varepsilon_O = D(X, O(X))
```

where `D` represents a measure of distance, distortion, or divergence between the phenomenon and its representation.

Intuitively:

```math
R(x) \neq x
```

or:

```math
R(x) = x - \varepsilon
```

This second formula is symbolic: in most cases `x` and `R(x)` do not even belong to the same mathematical space. Its value is conceptual: every finite representation leaves something of the phenomenon out.

Applied to consciousness, this observation implies that observable behavior, language, subjective reports, and neural measures do not automatically coincide with lived experience. They are representations, traces, or correlates of the conscious process.
