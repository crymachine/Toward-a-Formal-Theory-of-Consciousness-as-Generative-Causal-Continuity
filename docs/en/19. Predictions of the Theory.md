# 19. Predictions of the Theory

A scientific theory must produce risky predictions. The following are preliminary predictions.

## Prediction 1: loss of integration and loss of consciousness

If the causal integration of a system is drastically reduced while local activity is preserved, the degree of consciousness should decrease. This prediction is consistent with states such as anesthesia, deep sleep, coma, and functional disconnections.

## Prediction 2: purely feed-forward systems are not conscious in a strong sense

A purely feed-forward system, lacking recurrence, persistent memory, and self-modeling, could produce complex outputs without possessing strong consciousness. If it were demonstrated that a purely feed-forward system without memory possesses phenomenal experience, the theory would be strongly weakened.

## Prediction 3: agency increases the degree of consciousness

All else being equal in terms of integration and memory, a system capable of actively modifying its future inputs should manifest a higher degree of consciousness than a purely passive system.

## Prediction 4: self-modeling increases reflective consciousness

Systems with metacognitive capacities, error monitoring, and representation of their own state should manifest higher forms of consciousness than systems lacking a self-model.

## Prediction 5: external behavior is not enough

Two behaviorally equivalent systems can differ in the degree of consciousness if they differ in their internal causal organization. This prediction distinguishes the theory from pure behaviorism.

## Prediction 6: global consciousness requires local or global maxima of integration

A social group, a digital network, or the entire universe do not automatically become a conscious subject. They would need to display an integrated, bounded, recurrent, and self-modeling dynamic superior to the sum of subsystems.

## Prediction 7: aesthetic effectiveness and optimal loss region

In the aesthetic domain, overly determined works should leave less room for personal reconstruction, while overly indeterminate works should lose coupling capacity. One predicts an intermediate region in which informational loss maximizes resonance:

```math
\varepsilon_{min} < \varepsilon < \varepsilon_{max}
```

This prediction could be explored with studies on aesthetic ambiguity, musical pleasure, poetry, photography, abstract art, and individual differences among spectators.
